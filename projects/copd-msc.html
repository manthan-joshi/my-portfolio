Lung repair via gene-edited MSCs for COPD.

Grant proposal for the Stem Cells and Regeneration course. Reversing lung damage using SP-C gene-edited mesenchymal stem cells in a mouse model, combining CRISPR and AAV vectors.

← Back to all work
CourseStem Cells and Regeneration
WhereNortheastern
WhenFall 2024
FormatGrant proposal

COPD affects around 390 million people globally and kills over three million per year. Current treatments manage symptoms but don't reverse the underlying lung damage. The proposal asked a simple question: can gene-edited stem cells regenerate alveolar tissue?

The proposal

Surfactant Protein C (SP-C) is critical for alveolar type II cell function and surfactant production. In COPD and some interstitial lung diseases, SP-C function is compromised. The proposal was to use CRISPR/Cas9 to introduce a corrected SP-C gene into mesenchymal stem cells, deliver them via AAV, and test their ability to support alveolar regeneration in a mouse COPD model.

Design logic

  • MSCs are well-studied for tissue regeneration but their effect on lung repair in COPD has been inconsistent in the literature
  • Adding a gene-editing step gives the cells a defined therapeutic payload rather than relying on paracrine effects alone
  • AAV delivery for the gene edit keeps the system translatable to clinical models
  • Mouse elastase or cigarette smoke models were evaluated as the right preclinical readout

What I took from it

This was my first real exercise in writing a grant proposal rather than reading one. The hardest part wasn't the science. It was figuring out how to be specific enough to be credible without over-promising, and how to explain why this version of the idea was worth funding over other perfectly reasonable approaches.

A grant proposal is an argument in a specific genre. The science has to be right, but the argument has to be tight.
