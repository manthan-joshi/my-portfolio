CGT oversight under MHRA and Health Canada.

Graduate regulatory module comparing cell and gene therapy approval pathways in the UK and Canada, with detailed case studies on Kymriah and Abecma. Regulatory Landscapes coursework, fall 2025.

FocusUK and Canada CGT policy

CGT products don't win or lose on science alone. They win or lose on who regulates them, how payers evaluate them, and how long patients have to wait. This module was a side-by-side look at two jurisdictions that do it very differently.

Why UK and Canada

Both are advanced-economy jurisdictions with established CGT oversight, but they've made different structural choices. The UK integrates regulatory approval with economic review under the MHRA and NICE. Canada splits scientific approval at Health Canada from reimbursement, which creates a known delay between "approved" and "accessible."

What the module covered

  • MHRA pathways including ILAP (Innovative Licensing and Access Pathway), PIM, EAMS, and Rolling Review
  • Health Canada pathways including NDS, Priority Review, and NOC with Conditions (NOC/c)
  • Role of ethical oversight: REBs in Canada, Yellow Card Scheme for post-market safety in the UK
  • How each jurisdiction harmonizes (or doesn't) with FDA and EMA frameworks
  • Where each system creates bottlenecks for cell and gene therapy developers

Case study: Kymriah in the UK

Kymriah (tisagenlecleucel) is a CAR-T therapy for B-cell malignancies. In the UK, it's the canonical example of how MHRA scientific approval has to pass through NICE's health-economic review before NHS patients can access it. The coordination between those two bodies determines whether a therapy that works ever actually reaches a patient.

Case study: Abecma in Canada

Abecma (idecabtagene vicleucel) is a CAR-T for multiple myeloma, approved by Health Canada under NOC with Conditions. The case shows what happens when a therapy crosses the regulatory bar but hits reimbursement and provincial access barriers on the other side. Approved does not equal accessible.

The interesting policy question isn't "does it work?" It's "who decides if it works well enough to pay for, and how fast can they decide?"
