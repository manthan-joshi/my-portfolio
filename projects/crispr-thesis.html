Why this matters

CHO cells are the workhorse of biologics manufacturing, responsible for the majority of recombinant therapeutic proteins on the market. Being able to confidently edit them with CRISPR and verify the edit actually happened is foundational work for any biotech that wants to modify a production line, knock out a problematic gene, or introduce a new one.

HPRT1 is a classic selection marker locus. Edits at HPRT1 can be selected using 6-TG or HAT media, which made it the right choice for a project focused on methodology rather than therapeutic payload.

What I actually did

  • Designed the full experimental workflow for CRISPR/Cas9 editing at HPRT1, including guide selection and validation strategy
  • Prepared Cas9 ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complexes and transfected CHO cells using CRISPRmax
  • Ran the complete downstream validation: genomic DNA extraction, PCR amplification across the edit site, agarose gel electrophoresis, and Nanodrop quantification
  • Performed Sanger sequencing of PCR products and used ICE (Inference of CRISPR Edits) analysis to quantify INDEL rates
  • Ran the plasmid engineering side in parallel: bacterial transformation, colony screening, plasmid purification, and restriction enzyme digest verification using NotI and EcoRV
  • Managed the CHO cell culture throughout: seeding, passaging, cryopreservation, and puromycin selection optimized via CellTiter-Glo viability assay
Everyone can run a transfection. The value is in the downstream: what did you actually edit, and how confident are you that you edited it?

What I took from it

The biggest thing this project taught me was how much genome editing work is actually careful validation work. The edit is the easy part. Trusting that the edit happened, at the right place, with the expected frequency, is where real scientific discipline shows up.
