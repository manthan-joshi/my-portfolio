CodeButterfly

Healing butterfly skin, gene therapy for EB.

Class project on Epidermolysis Bullosa, a genetic condition where skin is as fragile as butterfly wings. Walked through the current CGT landscape and the first FDA-approved topical gene therapy for the disease.

FocusSkin CGT

Epidermolysis Bullosa (EB) is the kind of disease that makes the case for gene therapy by itself. Patients have skin that blisters from ordinary touch because of mutations in genes that make collagen VII and other structural proteins. Until recently there was no real therapeutic option.

The disease

EB is a group of inherited disorders where mutations disrupt proteins that anchor the epidermis to the dermis. Dystrophic EB, the most severe form, is caused by mutations in COL7A1, which encodes type VII collagen. The skin becomes mechanically fragile, blisters spontaneously, and in severe cases leads to chronic wounds, scarring, and increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma.

What the project covered

  • Pathophysiology of EB subtypes, focused on dystrophic EB and COL7A1
  • CRISPR-based approaches for correcting COL7A1 mutations in keratinocytes
  • Mesenchymal stem cell (MSC) therapy and its supporting evidence in EB
  • Vyjuvek (beremagene geperpavec), the first FDA-approved topical gene therapy for any disease, cleared in 2023 for DEB
  • Delivery challenges specific to skin: viral vector choice, dosing frequency, wound bed integration

Why Vyjuvek is a milestone

Vyjuvek is an HSV-1 based gene therapy delivered topically as a gel. It redoses because the edited keratinocytes are continuously sloughed off, which is a different mental model than a one-time systemic gene therapy. It also sets a regulatory precedent for topical gene therapies as a category, not just as a skin exception.

For the project I traced how Vyjuvek fits into the broader CGT pipeline, and what it means for other heritable skin conditions and for gene therapies that need to deal with tissue turnover.

Gene therapy isn't one shape. Vyjuvek doesn't look like Luxturna, and Luxturna doesn't look like Zolgensma. Matching the delivery mode to the biology of the tissue is the whole game.
