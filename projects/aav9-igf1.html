Muscle regeneration via AAV9–IGF-1.

Gene therapy proposal for delivering IGF-1 via AAV9 to support skeletal muscle regeneration while minimizing fibrosis. Activating growth factor expression post-injury to accelerate satellite cell-driven repair.

← Back to all work
CourseCell and Gene Therapies
WhereNortheastern
WhenFall 2024
TargetSkeletal muscle

Muscle heals, but it heals badly when the damage is severe. Fibrosis replaces functional tissue with scar, and the muscle comes back weaker. The proposal asked whether we could bias that balance back toward real regeneration using gene therapy.

The biology

Satellite cells are the resident stem cells of skeletal muscle. When a muscle is injured, they activate, proliferate, and fuse to repair damaged fibers. IGF-1 is one of the signaling molecules that drives this activation. The catch is that timing and local concentration matter. Too much IGF-1 or the wrong delivery mode and you get off-target effects.

Why AAV9

  • AAV9 has a well-characterized tropism for muscle tissue, including skeletal muscle
  • The immunogenicity profile is relatively favorable among AAV serotypes
  • It's already been used clinically in Zolgensma, which means the manufacturing and regulatory pathways are partially paved
  • For a muscle-specific payload, it's close to the obvious choice

The strategy

Deliver IGF-1 under a muscle-specific promoter via AAV9, timed to the post-injury window when satellite cells are activating. The hypothesis is that locally elevated IGF-1 biases the repair response toward regeneration rather than fibrotic scarring, producing muscle that's closer to functionally normal.

Precision, scalability, and translational potential. Better recovery shouldn't just be fast. It should be functional.
