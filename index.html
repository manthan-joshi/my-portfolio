01About § 01 / 04
The long version

I'm a biotechnologist with a deep interest in Cell and Gene Therapy. Drawn to the kind of science that doesn't just explain life, but tries to fix it at the molecular level.

My background is wet lab experience in CRISPR and molecular biology, paired with a growing interest in gene therapy strategy, regulatory thinking, and the business side of how therapies reach patients. I enjoy working at the intersection of science, structure, and storytelling.

The space between discovery and delivery.

That's what I'm actually working on, and trying to grow into.

My training started at MIT Manipal with a BTech in Biotechnology. My undergrad thesis at Tranalab in Bangalore taught me what a real research problem feels like. Two years at Reagene Biosciences followed, a 3D bioprinting startup where I watched science meet deadlines and grant cycles for the first time. That arc is why I came to Northeastern for the MS in Cell and Gene Therapies, and why I added the HBS Management Essentials certificate alongside it.

If you're building something exciting in biotech, CGT, or healthcare innovation, I'd love to connect. Always up for a good conversation, or a really sharp research idea.

02Direction § 02 / 04
Where I'm heading

The path I'm most interested in sits at the intersection of cell and gene therapy R&D and the business of how those therapies get built, evaluated, and delivered.

The roles have names that change depending on who's hiring. Business development, product strategy, translational science, regulatory affairs, medical affairs. The throughline is the same. Scientific rigor applied to decisions bigger than the bench.

I don't want to leave the lab entirely. The best translational thinkers I've met kept their hands on the science even as their titles drifted toward strategy. That's the model I'm building toward.

The HBS Management Essentials certificate, the CurcuNano pitch, the regulatory coursework on MHRA and Health Canada were not detours. They're the shape of what I want next.

03How I work § 03 / 04

Great results come from great teamwork. I'm ready to learn and driven to grow.

On the CurcuNano pitch team, I designed the Pipeline and Growth Story workstream. Six people had written their own slides with their own assumptions. My job wasn't to write the most polished individual slide. It was to read what everyone else had written and make sure the deck didn't contradict itself by slide 12. That's the part of teamwork I actually like.

Running the Graduate Biotech and Bioinfo Association as Vice President taught me the other side of it. A lot of team work isn't the flashy part. It's following up on the email nobody responded to, rebooking the room when the speaker cancels, remembering whose turn it is. Boring work that the event either has or it doesn't.

I'm motivated by clarity and momentum. When I understand the goal and what's needed, I move quickly, ask the right questions, and contribute where it matters most. Give me direction and context, and I'll bring focus, energy, and follow-through.

04Path so far § 04 / 04
What I keep learning

Looking across the projects so far, two things keep coming back. The work I've loved most is the kind where science has to translate into something else. A pitch, a protocol, a regulatory argument, a story a patient can follow. That translation is its own craft, and I'm still learning it.

The other thing is people. The mentors, PIs, and classmates who saw value in my contribution, even when I was still figuring out what that was, are the reason any of this moved forward. I hope I get to keep finding rooms full of people like that.
